Ngorongoro Crater
Ngorongoro Crater is a giant bowl-shaped valley (a volcanic caldera) in northern Tanzania, the largest intact one in the world. It formed when a volcano exploded and collapsed millions of years ago. It's inside the Ngorongoro Conservation Area, about ~180 km west of Arusha.
The name of the crater has an onomatopoeic origin; (it was named by the Maasai pastoralists after the sound produced by the cowbell (ngoro ngoro).) Based on fossil evidence OH 7 found at the Olduvai Gorge, various hominid species have occupied the area for at least . 1.75 million years
Big Five, large populations of zebras, wildebeest, hippos, and hyenas, as well as seasonal flamingos at Lake Magadi. Thanks to this rare combination of rich wildlife, stunning scenery, and easy animal viewing, Ngorongoro Crater is considered one of the best safari destinations in Africa.
